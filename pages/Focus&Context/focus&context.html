Focus & Context Methods

Visualizations for augmented objects are a well researched field to support medical doctors during laprascopic surgeries. In this kind of surgery, three small incisions are made into the patient's body and a camera and instruments are inserted. This camera captures images of the area of interest and is the only way for the surgeons to see the operation site.


However, the view is very limited, it is very hard to percieve the depth, and blood vessels can be covered by fatty tissue, or organs. This is very dangerous, as accidentally hurting a vessel might cause serious injuries. Therefore, augmented reality can be used to reveal vulnerable structures.


Just placing a virtual vessel on top of the laprascopic image is not enough, as the vessel appears to float above the surface and disrupt the spatial awareness. Thus, focus and context methods were introduced to blend the virtual world into the real world. They try to let the medical doctors focus on the important areas without occluding objects and losing the context.

The image captured by a camera during laprascopic surgery.

The virtual vessel is placed on top of the laprascopic image.

pq method

The images of a surgery have often many different features whereas the dominant color is mostly red. However, the many strong edges between different types of tissue and small wrinkels give enough contrast. The pq method by Lerotic et al. [1] uses those features by extracting the edges with a Sobel filter. This image of the feature extraction is called the pq space.

The image captured by a camera during laprascopic surgery.

The pq space of the laprascopic image. The result of the Sobel filter.

The virtual vessel is placed behind the features of the pq space.

The pq space can again be merged with the laprascopic image to keep color information. Additionally, the artifacts from tiny color changes are not important for the perception effect. Therefore, a gaussian filter can be used to blur the source image and small values from the pq space can be omitted by a threshold. Thus, the background is still visible and only prominant features are highlighted and drawn over the vessel to create occlusion cues.

window effect

A very common focus and context method is to us a virtual window to achieve a see-through effect for occluded objects - for example used by Bichlmeier et al. This shifts the focus onto the virtual objects. However, by removing the parts in front of the augmented objects, important features of the real world are missing, too.

A window with sharp edges to seperate virtual objects and the real camera image.

A window with a smooth transition between real and virtual world.

Conclusion

Both focus and context methods blend virtual objects with the real world however, they shift the focus on different features. The window method gives a clear view onto virtual objects by cutting information of the real world. Whereas the pq method tries to keep the most important features of the patient's body and slightly occludes the augmented object.

A combination of those techniques is therefore very common [1]. The extracted features of the laprascopic image can be drawn on top of the augmented object and the window.
